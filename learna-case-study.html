Case study The designer's portfolio

Project brief

To Create a Portfolio That Clearly Displays My Skills, Process and Personality

Probably the most important product I'll build this year, my aim was to:

  • build a digital UX portfolio
  • enable users to quickly find information about my design process
  • show examples of my product design work
  • enable hirers to contact me easily and invite me for an interview!

Solution:

I created a responsive, mobile-first website using bootstrap, my design process was based on the design thinking methodology and its 5 stages: empathise, define, ideate, prototype and test. Users have found it very easy to navigate the site and complete scenario-based tasks in the latest iteration of the portfolio.

My Role:

I was solely responsible for all stages of the development process from initial secondary research to the creation of the final website and visuals.

Challenges overcome:

As a personal project with zero budget the main challenge I faced was to research the user requirements without having the means to visit Hiring mangers and UX professionals for interviews/contextual enquiries.

Process:

I followed a design thinking process in designing the portfolio, with the clear distinct stages 'Empathise', 'Define', 'Ideate', 'Prototype' and 'Test'. This was an iterative process, but for clarity I've displayed the content under the process headings in this case study.

Empathise:

Research:

Hiring managers are very vocal and offer a wealth of information when building your portfolio, especially if face-to-face interviews aren't feasible!

The function of the portfolio is straightforward, focusing on a few tasks, so I felt that considering each manager's content as an 'interview' should show any behaviour patterns or common requirements. A little secondary research lead to a vast amount of content that answered any questions I had about the user requirements and revealed that:

  • UX hiring managers want case studies to tell a story - design is about storytelling.
  • A portfolio should contain 2-3 case studies of my best and varied work.
  • My portfolio should display my personality and be informally, but professionally, written to stand out.
  • UX hiring managers feel that how the portfolio is constructed will reflect a lot about me as a designer - no room for sloppiness!

I decided on a baseline of 5 'interviews', if I hadn't seen a clear pattern then I'd undertake further research or may have needed to rethink my strategyâ€¦ fortunately I found that the requirements and responses were very similar among the 5 'interviewees'.

Reyna's story

"I do have a designated desk in the office - but I rarely get time to sit at it! I seem to be constantly in meetings... I find I often have to squeeze in a few minutes here and there on my phone to view and earmark the portfolios of potential new hires.

As a company we're passionate about design and the product journey, so I find I'll often discuss some of the best portfolios with my colleagues (we're all about collaboration) and show them the stand-out examples over lunch or a quickly-grabbed coffee.

I get a real buzz when I come across a portfolio that shows that the designer just 'gets it' and produces really good work ... especially if it doesn't read like a user's manual! I start to see how they could make a valuable contribution to our design teams."

Ideate

I started with a card-based layout for the portfolio, but it will never be bursting with content and seemed to leave a lot of wasted space on desktop.

I also considered a very trendy dark mode, but felt that this works best when you want the images to take centre stage and better suited for a photographer's portfolio, mine is content-driven.

I considered (and finally adopted) a bottom tab bar for navigation on mobile and more conventional navigation on desktop.

This was a very minimal one-page design that I discarded pretty early in the process.

I thought that constant scrolling with no top-level navigation would make for a poorer user experience; users could easily lose track of their position on the page and there was no quick way to access particular information.

This was another single page design, but with top-level navigation to help the user find their way around the page and locate the relevant content quickly.

I considered leaving a column on the left for titles and headers at larger screen sizes, but felt that smaller sections may look a little cluttered.

Also I felt that I'd lose the visual hierarchy and the design would be less clear in sections that expand to make the most of the whole screen.

This is closer to the final design, content is generally contained in 2 centred columns on larger devices, but allows for hero images to make the most of screen space where needed without losing the visual hierarchy.

Takeaway

Lessons learnt:

  • Secondary research can provide a wealth of information about the user in some circumstances, but of course I had to rely on them 'practicing what they preach', and there wasn't an opportunity to observe the hiring mangers' actual behaviour.
  • 'Mixed' fidelity prototypes only confuse the user, in future I'll create a low fidelity prototype initially to test that the flow makes sense to the user, before later adopting a high fidelity prototype of the almost complete product design to carry out scenario-based user testing.
  • Based on the research findings it was important to keep the portfolio content-driven. When making design decisions I needed remember that I am not the target user – hiring managers won't thank me for being too original in my portfolio design if it makes it more difficult to find the relevant information.
  • The bottom tab bar mobile pattern is good to adopt for ease of use on larger mobile devices, but I must be mindful that some users may not expect the navigation of a webpage to be at the bottom. Use a little animation and interactions to ensure that the user's made aware of the navigation.

Future developments:

  • I'll research and perform user-testing on tablet to determine at what screen size the bottom tab bar stops being useful – testing was undertaken on mobile devices for this case study.
